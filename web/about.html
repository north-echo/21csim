What is 21csim?

21csim is a Monte Carlo counterfactual simulation of the 21st century. It models 303 pivotal event nodes -- from the 2000 U.S. election to speculative far-future milestones -- as a directed acyclic graph (DAG). Each node branches into multiple possible outcomes with assigned probabilities. When you run a simulation, the engine rolls the dice at every branch point, cascading the consequences through the dependency graph to produce one plausible alternate history.

Run it thousands of times with different random seeds and you get a probability distribution over possible 21st centuries -- some optimistic, some catastrophic, most somewhere in between.

How It Works

  • 303 event nodes organized as a DAG with explicit dependency edges
  • Probabilistic branching: each node has 2-6 outcome branches with assigned probabilities that sum to 1.0
  • Cascading dependencies: the outcome of one node can shift the probabilities or gate the existence of downstream nodes
  • Conditional gating: some nodes only fire if a specific upstream branch was taken (e.g., a war node gated on a diplomacy-failure branch)
  • 32 world-state dimensions: each branch adjusts dimensions like democracy_index, climate_stability, technological_progress, and global_cooperation
  • Composite scoring: after all nodes resolve, the 32 dimensions are weighted and combined into a single composite score that maps to a verdict

What It Is NOT

  • Not a prediction. The simulation explores structured possibilities, not forecasts. No outcome is presented as likely to happen.
  • Not political commentary. Nodes model events across the political spectrum without editorial judgment.
  • Not comprehensive history. 303 nodes cannot capture every significant event. The selection focuses on high-leverage inflection points with clear branching structure.

Verdict Classification

After a simulation run resolves all 303 nodes, the accumulated world-state dimensions produce a composite score. That score maps to one of eight verdict categories:

Verdict Composite Range Meaning
Golden Age ≥ 75 Broad flourishing across most dimensions: strong democracy, climate stability, technological benefit, global cooperation
Transcendence ≥ 85 + tech Technological transformation so profound that conventional metrics break down -- e.g., superintelligence or post-scarcity
Progress 55 - 74 Meaningful improvement in most areas, though with setbacks and unresolved problems
Muddling Through 40 - 54 Incremental change, mixed results. Some things improve, others worsen, net effect ambiguous
Decline 25 - 39 Measurable deterioration in key dimensions: rising authoritarianism, worsening climate, economic stagnation
Catastrophe 10 - 24 Severe collapse in multiple dimensions: major wars, civilizational regression, widespread suffering
Extinction < 10 Civilizational or literal extinction scenarios: runaway AI, nuclear winter, ecological collapse
Radically Different High divergence High divergence ratio with ambiguous composite score -- the world is so different from baseline that "better" or "worse" loses meaning

Probability Calibration

The base probabilities assigned to each branch are subjective estimates informed by historical analysis, not statistical predictions derived from data. They are calibrated so that batch runs produce realistic distributions -- i.e., the most common verdicts across thousands of seeds roughly match the range of plausible 21st-century trajectories.

"Realistic" here means the simulation should not systematically produce utopias or catastrophes. The probabilities are tuned so that historical branches dominate when they should, while leaving meaningful room for divergence at genuine inflection points.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this a prediction?

No. 21csim is a structured exploration of counterfactual possibilities. It makes no claims about what will happen -- only about what could happen given a set of modeled branch points. Think of it as a tool for thinking about uncertainty, not a crystal ball.

How are probabilities determined?

Each branch probability is a subjective estimate informed by historical analysis, domain expertise, and expert intuition. They are then calibrated so that batch runs across thousands of seeds produce plausible distributions of outcomes. For historical nodes (pre-2025), the "historical" branch typically has high probability, reflecting that history actually happened. For future nodes, probabilities reflect genuine uncertainty.

Can I add my own nodes?

Yes. Create a YAML file in src/csim/data/nodes/ following the existing schema. Each node needs an id, year_month, title, description, domain, distribution with outcome probabilities, and an outcomes block defining each branch's effects on world-state dimensions. You can also specify dependencies and conditional gating.

Why does event X have probability Y?

Every probability is a calibration choice, not a scientific measurement. The philosophy is: historical branches should be highly probable (the past happened), near-future branches should reflect current trends and expert consensus, and far-future branches should acknowledge deep uncertainty. The goal is a simulation that feels plausible in aggregate, even if individual estimates are debatable. Disagreement about any specific probability is expected and welcome -- it is the whole point of counterfactual reasoning.

How is the composite score calculated?

The simulation tracks 32 world-state dimensions (e.g., democracy_index, climate_stability, economic_equality). Each branch outcome adds or subtracts from these dimensions. At the end of a run, the dimensions are combined with importance weights into a single composite score ranging from 0 to 100. The score then maps to one of eight verdict categories.

What does "Radically Different" mean?

A "Radically Different" verdict occurs when the simulation produces a world with a high divergence ratio -- meaning many nodes took non-historical branches -- combined with an ambiguous composite score (neither clearly good nor clearly bad). In these timelines, the world has changed so fundamentally from our own that conventional notions of "better" or "worse" lose their meaning.
